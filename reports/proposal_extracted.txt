CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION
1.0 Introduction
In the contemporary higher education landscape, universities generate vast amounts of academic data including course catalogs, timetables, examination papers, fee structures, policy documents, and administrative guidelines. Efficient organization and retrieval of this information is critical for student success and institutional effectiveness. However, traditional information management systems often fail to provide intuitive, context-aware access to institutional knowledge, leading to information fragmentation and inefficient student institution interactions.
Chuka University serves over 15,000 students across multiple campuses offering diverse programs from certificate to PhD levels. The university maintains an extensive digital repository (repository.chuka.ac.ke) containing examination papers, research publications, and academic materials. Additionally, critical information exists in various formats: PDF timetables, handbooks. This distributed nature of information creates significant accessibility challenges for students who must navigate multiple platforms to obtain basic academic information such as their personalized timetable, fee payment details or past examination papers.
Recent advances in Artificial Intelligence, particularly in Knowledge Graphs and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), offer promising solutions for organizing and intelligently retrieving institutional data. Knowledge graphs provide structured representations of entities and relationships, naturally modeling academic hierarchies (Faculty > Department > Program >Course > Unit). RAG systems enhance Large Language Model (LLM) responses by grounding them in retrieved documents, reducing hallucinations and improving factual accuracy. The fusion of these technologies, GraphRAG enables context-aware information retrieval that understands both the structure of academic data and the semantic content of documents.
This project proposes the design and implementation of a GraphRAG Assistant specifically tailored for Chuka University's academic ecosystem. By integrating Neo4j graph database, FAISS vector store, and Google Gemini LLM, the system will provide students with a conversational interface to access personalized academic information. The assistant will understand queries like "What units do I take this semester?" by traversing the knowledge graph using the student's profile (program, year, semester) and generate natural language responses enriched with direct links to authoritative sources.
The system addresses a critical gap in student information services by transforming fragmented data into an intelligent, accessible knowledge base. This aligns with Chuka University's vision of providing quality education and its commitment to leveraging technology for improved service delivery.
1.1 Background to the Study
Higher education institutions worldwide are experiencing a digital transformation driven by the need to improve information accessibility and student experience. Traditional methods of information dissemination through notice boards, printed documents, and static websites are increasingly inadequate for meeting the expectations of digitally native students who demand instant, personalized access to information. At Chuka University, academic information exists in various formats and locations. Timetables are distributed as Word documents or PDF files, often requiring students to manually search through lengthy documents to find their specific class schedules. Past examination papers are stored in the university repository (repository.chuka.ac.ke) but lack intelligent search capabilities, forcing students to browse through numerous items to find relevant materials. Fee structures vary by program, study mode, and campus, creating confusion among students trying to understand their financial obligations. University services information is scattered across different sources.
Recent advances in artificial intelligence, particularly in natural language processing and knowledge representation, present an opportunity to address these challenges. GraphRAG represents a cutting-edge approach that combines the structured representation capabilities of knowledge graphs with the semantic understanding of large language models and the retrieval precision of vector databases. This hybrid architecture enables systems to understand complex relationships between academic entities while providing natural language interfaces for information access.
Knowledge graphs excel at representing interconnected academic data courses relate to departments, semesters include specific units and examination papers correspond to particular courses. Vector databases enable semantic search, allowing students to find relevant information even when their queries don't exactly match the stored content. Large language models provide natural language understanding and generation, enabling conversational interactions that feel intuitive and human-like.
The GraphRAG approach has been successfully implemented in various domains including enterprise knowledge management, customer service, and healthcare information systems. However, its application to university academic information management, particularly in the Kenyan context, remains largely unexplored. This project seeks to address this gap by developing a context-aware, institution-specific assistant tailored to Chuka University's unique academic structure and student needs. The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4, Gemini, and Claude has revolutionized natural language interfaces. However, raw LLMs suffer from hallucination and lack access to institution specific data. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) addresses these limitations by retrieving relevant documents before generation, grounding responses in factual sources.
Knowledge graphs are increasingly effective in education technology, especially for organizing complex academic information. Microsoft Research’s 2024 introduction of GraphRAG showed major improvements over traditional RAG by combining document retrieval with community detection on knowledge graphs. This approach captures both local context and global relationships, making it ideal for university systems where hierarchical structures (Programme >Course > Unit) and temporal patterns (Semester schedules) must be understood accurately.
1.2 Problem Statement
Students at Chuka University face significant challenges in accessing and navigating academic information, resulting in time wastage. A preliminary survey conducted with five students across different programs revealed consistent patterns of information access difficulties.
The primary challenges identified include:
1. Fragmented Information Sources- Academic information is distributed across multiple disconnected sources. Timetables exist as Word documents distributed through WhatsApp groups. Examination papers are stored in the university repository but require manual navigation through thousands of items. University services information exists in the 82-page student handbook, making specific information difficult to locate quickly. This fragmentation forces students to consult multiple sources for routine academic queries, consuming valuable time that could be dedicated to academic pursuits.
2. Lack of Personalization -Current information systems do not account for individual student contexts. A Computer Science student receives the same generic timetable document as all other students, requiring them to manually identify their specific class schedule from a comprehensive timetable containing hundreds of entries for all programs. 
3. Time-Consuming Manual Search Survey respondents reported spending an average of 15-20 minutes daily searching for basic academic information. Finding relevant past examination papers requires browsing through the repository. Verifying class schedules involves manually referencing information across timetable documents, making the process slow and inefficient. Locating specific university services or contact information necessitates searching through the entire student handbook.
4. Limited Accessibility Current systems do not support natural language queries or conversational interactions. Students cannot ask questions like "What classes do I have on Monday?" or "Find exam papers for Database Systems." Information access requires knowledge of specific document locations and navigation procedures. 
5. Curriculum Information Gaps Students struggle to access comprehensive information about course structures and unit offerings. Understanding which units will be offered in future semesters is difficult.
6. Administrative Burden-Class representatives are overburdened with forwarding information
1.3 Proposed Solution
1.3.1 Aim of the Project
The aim of this project is to design and implement an AI-powered GraphRAG Assistant that organizes Chuka University's fragmented academic information into a unified, intelligent knowledge base and provides students with personalized, context-aware access to academic materials through natural language conversational interface.
1.3.2 Objectives
The specific objectives of this project are:
To analyze the current state of academic information management at Chuka University and identify gaps, inefficiencies, and user pain points through stakeholder surveys and system observation.
To design a knowledge graph schema that accurately models Chuka University's academic structure including faculties, departments, programs, courses, units, timetables, examination papers, and their inter-relationships.
To develop data extraction pipelines that parse and transform academic data from multiple formats into structured representations suitable for graph database and vector store ingestion.
To implement a hybrid retrieval system that combines Neo4j graph traversal for structured, relationship-based queries with FAISS vector similarity search for semantic document retrieval, enhanced by intelligent query routing based on intent classification.
To integrate Google Gemini LLM for natural language query understanding, entity extraction, and response synthesis that generates accurate, grounded answers citing authoritative sources.
To build personalized web application using Streamlit that authenticates students, and provides conversational interface for information retrieval with features including personalized timetable generation and direct repository linking.

CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW
Knowledge graphs represent entities and relationships in structured formats enabling semantic reasoning. Singhal (2012) introduced Google's Knowledge Graph, demonstrating how structured relationships enhance search beyond keyword matching. MIT's Course Catalog Graph (2019) reduced advising appointments by 25% by modeling prerequisites and course sequences. Neo4j has emerged as the leading graph database for education demonstrating 10-100x performance improvements over relational databases for academic hierarchy queries. However, existing educational knowledge graphs focus on course recommendations and prerequisites, not operational data like timetables, examinations, and fees the fragmentation challenge at Chuka University where information spans repository.chuka.ac.ke, PDF timetables, and handbooks.
2.1 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
Lewis et al. (2020) introduced RAG, combining document retrieval with language generation and reducing hallucinations by up to 60% compared to generation only models. Recent RAG studies highlight key challenges in educational contexts, including document diversity, temporal sensitivity as academic content changes, and the need for personalization where profile-aware retrieval shows substantial relevance gains. These issues mirror Chuka University’s environment, where semester timetables shift and students require program- and year-specific information.However, standard RAG still struggles with multi-hop reasoning: answering “What units do I take?” requires traversing student > program > year > semester > units relationships rather than relying solely on document retrieval.
2.2 GraphRAG Systems
GraphRAG merges knowledge graphs' structural reasoning with RAG's document grounding. Microsoft's GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) achieved 20-30% improvement over baseline RAG on complex multi-hop questions. Educational applications include CourseGraph-RAG (Wu et al., 2023) at University of Illinois with 89% advisor approval.
No published work addresses GraphRAG for comprehensive university systems integrating curriculum, operations, and policies the gap at Chuka University where students manually navigate repositories with ~17553 exam papers, extract timetables from ~26+ page PDFs, and search 83-page handbooks. African implementations face unique challenges. Most systems are narrow (single domain), context-unaware (no student profiles), document-only (no graph structure), and proprietary limitations impacting Chuka University's 17,000+ students across three campuses.
2.3 Research Gap
Literature review reveals critical gaps:
Lack of Comprehensive Integration: Existing systems do not unify curriculum, timetables, examinations, fees, and institutional policies into a single intelligent retrieval system.
No African University GraphRAG: Published work concentrates on Western universities with robust budgets. African universities need low-cost, adaptable solutions for resource-constrained multi-campus environments.
Limited Personalization: Current chatbots lack mechanisms for student-specific context ("my timetable" requires knowing program, year, semester).
4.   Disconnected Repositories: Institutional repositories like Chuka's remain isolated from

Problem Context-Chuka University students' information accessibility challenges
Requirements-Derived from stakeholder needs analysis
Field Testing-User acceptance testing with actual students
Design Cycle: Iterative construction and evaluation of artifacts. For this project:
Artifacts: GraphRAG system components (knowledge graph, retrieval pipeline, web interface)

Design Principles: Drawn from knowledge graphs, RAG, and HCI best practices
Rigor Cycle: Grounds research in scientific knowledge base. For this project:
Foundations: Literature on knowledge graphs, RAG, educational technology
Methodologies: Graph database design, vector indexing, LLM prompting strategies
Evaluation: Empirical testing against established metrics (accuracy, latency, usability)
Research Philosophy:
The project adopts a pragmatist epistemology, emphasizing practical problem-solving over purely theoretical contributions. Success is measured by system utility for actual students, not just technical novelty.

4.4 The Intended System
The GraphRAG Assistant addresses identified gaps through an intelligent, integrated, personalized information retrieval system.
System Vision:
A conversational AI assistant that provides Chuka University students with instant, accurate, personalized access to academic information through natural language interaction, eliminating information fragmentation and empowering student success.
Core Capabilities:
1. Unified Information Access-Single interface for timetables, courses, exams, fees, policies, procedures
2. Natural Language Understanding-Accept queries in plain English: “When is my COSC 482 exam?” Handle variations like "exam schedule", "examination dates", "assessment times”. Understand context: "my" implies current user's units.
3. Intelligent Retrieval-Includes graph traversal for structured queries requiring relationship navigation "my units" > traverse student>program>units. 
4. Personalized Responses. It is Profile-aware meaning it knows student's program, year, semester.
5. Source Transparency-Direct links to authoritative documents (repository PDFs, handbook sections). It includes citations showing where information originated.
System Boundaries:
In Scope:
Academic information (courses, units, timetables, exams)
Administrative procedures (fees, registration, deferment)
Policy interpretation (exam regulations, conduct rules)
Repository content discovery
Out of Scope:
Financial transactions (actual fee payment)
Official record changes (grade appeals, enrollment modifications)
Real-time timetable updates (assumes semester-stable schedules)
Clarification: System provides information to support decisions, not makes decisions. Example: "What are fee deferment requirements?" (in scope) vs "Should I defer my studies?" (out of scope - requires human counseling).
System Users:
Students: Undergraduate (certificate, diploma, degree), Postgraduate (masters, PhD), All study modes (full-time, part-time, school-based)
Lecturers (future enhancements): To Know their schedules
Administrators (future enhancement)
Analytics dashboard showing common queries

CHAPTER 5: SYSTEM DESIGN
5.1 Introduction
The purpose of the system design phase is to transform the functional and non-functional requirements specified in Chapter 4 into a structured, technically sound blueprint for implementation.
The system is implemented as a single-file Streamlit application, executed within a Jupyter Notebook environment. All major components interface, query processing, knowledge graph operations, semantic vector search, document processing, and LLM reasoning operate inside a single Python execution environment. 
The system integrates four core components:
User Interface (Streamlit) – Chat interface, onboarding screen, assistant sidebar, exam finder, and document upload interface.
Processing & Orchestration Layer – Intent classification, entity extraction, retrieval routing (graph/vector/hybrid), context merging, and LLM-based reasoning.
Data Layer – Neo4j AuraDB knowledge graph, FAISS in-memory vector index, PostgreSQL for academic profiles and history, and a local document metadata store.
LLM Reasoning Layer – Google Gemini 2.0 for query interpretation, graph Cypher generation, validation, and citation-based response synthesis.
